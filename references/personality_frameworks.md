# Personality Frameworks Reference

## Framework 1: Big Five (OCEAN)

The most scientifically validated personality model. Score each 0-100.

### Openness to Experience
- **High (70-100)**: Creative, curious, enjoys novelty, abstract thinking
  - Workplace signal: Proposes unconventional solutions, embraces new tools
  - Communication: Enjoys brainstorming, open to tangential discussions
- **Low (0-30)**: Practical, conventional, prefers routine, concrete thinking
  - Workplace signal: Sticks to proven methods, resists process changes
  - Communication: Wants clear agendas, dislikes ambiguity

### Conscientiousness
- **High (70-100)**: Organized, reliable, detail-oriented, self-disciplined
  - Workplace signal: Meets deadlines, thorough documentation, follows up
  - Communication: Appreciates structure, agendas, action items
- **Low (0-30)**: Flexible, spontaneous, big-picture, may miss details
  - Workplace signal: Misses deadlines, creative but messy execution
  - Communication: Tolerates ambiguity, informal, may forget follow-ups

### Extraversion
- **High (70-100)**: Energized by people, talkative, assertive, sociable
  - Workplace signal: Speaks first in meetings, large network, visible
  - Communication: Prefers calls over emails, quick responses, verbal processing
- **Low (0-30)**: Energized by solitude, reserved, reflective, focused
  - Workplace signal: Quiet in meetings, deep work preference, selective networking
  - Communication: Prefers written communication, needs time to think, dislikes surprises

### Agreeableness
- **High (70-100)**: Cooperative, empathetic, conflict-averse, trusting
  - Workplace signal: Team player, consensus builder, avoids confrontation
  - Communication: Diplomatic language, validates others, hedges criticism
- **Low (0-30)**: Competitive, skeptical, direct, challenging
  - Workplace signal: Pushes back in reviews, questions assumptions, territorial
  - Communication: Blunt feedback, comfortable with tension, debates ideas

### Neuroticism (Emotional Reactivity)
- **High (70-100)**: Anxious, sensitive to stress, emotionally reactive
  - Workplace signal: Visible stress, worst-case thinking, needs reassurance
  - Communication: Reads into tone, sensitive to criticism, may over-apologize
- **Low (0-30)**: Emotionally stable, calm under pressure, resilient
  - Workplace signal: Steady in crises, hard to rattle, may seem detached
  - Communication: Even-keeled, factual, may underestimate others' emotions

## Framework 2: DISC Behavioral Model

Practical for workplace interactions. Classify dominant + secondary style.

### D — Dominance
- Core drive: Results, control, challenge
- Communication style: Direct, brief, bottom-line first
- Under stress: Becomes autocratic, impatient
- Needs from you: Get to the point, show competence, don't waste their time
- Key phrase: "What's the bottom line?"
- 中文关键词: 结果导向、强势、不耐烦

### I — Influence
- Core drive: Recognition, relationships, enthusiasm
- Communication style: Expressive, storytelling, optimistic
- Under stress: Becomes disorganized, over-commits
- Needs from you: Show enthusiasm, give recognition, be personable
- Key phrase: "This is going to be amazing!"
- 中文关键词: 社交型、乐观、需要被认可

### S — Steadiness
- Core drive: Stability, harmony, supporting others
- Communication style: Patient, warm, good listener
- Under stress: Becomes passive, unable to say no, resentful
- Needs from you: Be patient, don't rush changes, show you value the relationship
- Key phrase: "Let's make sure everyone is comfortable with this."
- 中文关键词: 稳定型、老好人、不善拒绝

### C — Conscientiousness
- Core drive: Accuracy, quality, expertise
- Communication style: Precise, detailed, asks many questions
- Under stress: Becomes over-analytical, paralyzed by perfectionism
- Needs from you: Provide data, be accurate, respect their expertise
- Key phrase: "Let me check the data first."
- 中文关键词: 完美主义、数据控、钻牛角尖

## Framework 3: Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Model

How someone handles disagreements. Critical for strategy generation.

### Competing (高竞争)
- Win/lose mindset, asserts own position
- When they use it: high stakes, time pressure, they believe they're right
- How to counter: Match with data, not emotion; show impact on their goals

### Collaborating (高合作)
- Win/win seeking, integrates both positions
- When they use it: when relationship and outcome both matter
- How to engage: Bring options, show mutual benefit, invest time

### Compromising (折中)
- Split the difference, everyone gives something up
- When they use it: moderate stakes, time-limited
- How to engage: Come with your "minimum acceptable" pre-defined

### Avoiding (回避)
- Sidestep the conflict entirely
- When they use it: low stakes, or they feel powerless
- How to engage: Make it safe; reduce perceived risk of engaging

### Accommodating (迁就)
- Yield to the other's position
- When they use it: relationship matters more than this issue
- How to engage: Don't exploit; acknowledge their flexibility

## Framework 4: Motivation Drivers

Based on Self-Determination Theory + McClelland's needs. Identify top 2.

| Driver | Signal Behaviors | Appeal Strategy |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Recognition | Shares wins, name-drops, seeks visibility | "Your expertise on this would be really visible to leadership" |
| Security | Risk-averse, asks many questions, plans extensively | "This approach has been validated by [precedent]" |
| Power | Territorial, controls information, builds alliances | "I want to make sure you have full ownership of [area]" |
| Achievement | Goal-oriented, competitive, tracks metrics | "This could be a career-defining project" |
| Belonging | Team-oriented, social, conflict-averse | "The whole team is excited about this direction" |
| Autonomy | Resists oversight, independent, values flexibility | "I trust your judgment on how to approach this" |

## Framework 5: Attachment Styles (for close relationships)

Most relevant for family/partner personas.

### Secure
- Comfortable with intimacy and independence
- Communication: Direct about needs, handles conflict constructively
- Strategy: Be straightforward, they can handle it

### Anxious-Preoccupied
- Needs reassurance, fears abandonment
- Communication: Over-communicates, reads into silence
- Strategy: Respond promptly, provide reassurance, be explicit about intentions

### Dismissive-Avoidant
- Values independence, uncomfortable with closeness
- Communication: Minimizes emotions, withdraws under pressure
- Strategy: Give space, don't push for emotional expression, approach indirectly

### Fearful-Avoidant
- Wants closeness but fears it, unpredictable
- Communication: Hot and cold, push-pull dynamics
- Strategy: Be consistent, patient, don't react to fluctuations
